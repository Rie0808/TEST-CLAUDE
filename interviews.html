In their own words

Interviews with three caregivers.

Lightly edited for clarity. Names, identifying details, and place names have been generalised where requested.

Jocelyn

"By the time I got him to TCM I was already exhausted."

Caregiver to her father-in-law. They live apart.

Walk us through the TCM trip you mentioned.

My FIL called me on a Tuesday afternoon. He said he wanted to go to "that TCM near Hougang." That was the whole brief. So I asked him — which block? Which row of shops? Near which coffee shop? He couldn't really say. He just kept describing the entrance, like, "Got a yellow signboard, then go in got staircase."

What did you do next?

I went on Google. I tried "TCM Hougang" and got like fifteen results. Then I started doing Street View around the area he described. I was looking for a yellow signboard. This took maybe twenty minutes. Eventually I called my husband at work and he remembered the name — apparently his dad had been there before.

And then?

Then I called Grab. Then the driver came, and my FIL got in, and the driver wasn't sure of the exact unit, and they kept calling me. I'm at work, by the way. So I'm running between my desk and the corridor, telling the driver "no, it's the next block," telling my FIL "wait for the driver to call you." The trip was maybe twelve minutes. The whole episode was over an hour of my afternoon.

How often does this kind of thing happen?

Honestly? Two, three times a week. Not always TCM. Sometimes the wet market, sometimes my MIL's friend's place. Always a place they know in their head but I have to find for them.

What would have helped most that day?

If the place was just already saved somewhere. Like — once I know where FIL's TCM is, I should never have to find it again. Why am I starting from scratch every time?

Julie

"For thirty minutes, twice a day, my life just stops."

Caregiver to her mother (Aunty G), who has early-stage dementia.

Tell us about your mum's daily routine.

My mum lives in her own place. Her part-time helper comes in the morning, cleans, makes sure mum is dressed and fed. Then around ten, the helper walks her down to the lobby and pings me. That's when my day stops.

What happens in the next thirty minutes?

I call Grab. While I'm waiting for the driver to accept, I'm watching my mum's WhatsApp location to make sure she hasn't wandered. When the driver accepts, I message him — long message — "my mother has early-stage dementia, please confirm pickup with me before she gets in, she may not remember exactly where she's going." Then I monitor the car the whole ride. About five minutes before arrival, I message my own helper at my place: "she's coming, please go down now." If my helper is late, my mum is standing alone outside my building. I cannot let that happen.

What's the hardest part?

The driver briefing. Every single trip, I'm retyping the same paragraph. Sometimes I copy-paste from an old chat. But each driver is different, so I'm always slightly editing it. And I'm always anxious that they won't read it before they pick her up.

And in the evening?

Same thing in reverse. My helper walks her down at my place, I book the Grab, brief the driver, monitor the route, ping the part-time helper at her place to receive her. Twice a day. Five days a week. I cannot do any meetings between 10 and 10:30, or 6 and 6:30.

Have you tried anything else?

I thought about hiring a private driver, but the cost is wild. I thought about asking my mum to take a taxi from the stand, but she gets confused with the destinations. I thought about asking the helpers to talk to each other, but they don't have WhatsApp on the same group, and frankly I don't trust the part-time helper to handle if something goes wrong on the ride. So it stays with me.

If you could change one thing tomorrow?

Make me not the messenger. I don't need to be the one telling everyone what to do. I just need to know if something goes wrong.

HuiN

"I'm not just booking a Grab. I'm choreographing two arrivals."

Caregiver to her father, who has many medical appointments. She meets her parents at each clinic.

How many appointments does your dad have in a month?

Right now? Cardiologist, endocrinologist, polyclinic for medication review, physio, and the dental check-up I keep deferring. So probably six or seven scheduled things per month. Plus the unexpected ones.

Why can't your mum coordinate?

She tries. But she gets the dates mixed up, and she can't really use Grab on her own. She used to be the one organising everything in the family, so it's hard for both of us. I keep the calendar; she comes along to the appointments.

Walk us through a typical appointment day.

Let's say the appointment is at 3pm at the polyclinic. The day before, I message my parents to remind them. On the day, around 2pm, I check traffic and decide when to book their Grab. I send it. I message my dad: "Grab coming, please wait downstairs at 2:30." Then I have to leave my own work to make my way to the polyclinic. I usually take a Grab too. I'm doing math in my head — if I leave at 2:25, will I get there before them? Can I be at the entrance when they arrive?

Why does the rendezvous matter so much?

Because if they arrive first and I'm not there, my dad will try to walk in by himself, get to the wrong floor, panic. My mum can't really help with the registration. So me being there at the door — that's not a nice-to-have, it's the whole point. If I'm late, the appointment falls apart.

Do you ever miss?

A few times. Once their Grab took a faster route than mine and they were standing in the lobby for fifteen minutes. My dad called me four times. I felt terrible.

If something could just handle the math for you?

That would be huge. I don't want to be the one estimating traffic and sequencing the bookings. I just want to be at the door when they arrive.
