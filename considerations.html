Design considerations

How we got to Carebear.

After we mapped the pain Jocelyn, Julie, and HuiN were carrying, we didn't jump straight to building. We looked at the obvious answers first — and worked out why each fell short. This is that thinking.

01

Why not just add a feature inside Grab?

The most direct fix — and the one we can't ship.

The cleanest version of this product lives natively inside Grab: a "Care mode" with saved care recipients, recurring trips, driver briefing cards, and multi-party tracking built in. But we don't work at Grab, and Grab won't let an external team ship into their app.

Our move: build the caregiver-side experience standalone, prove that the demand and the design are real, and pitch the native integration to Grab as the obvious next step. The standalone is the wedge; the native version is the win.

02

Why not a shared family calendar?

Google Calendar / Apple Calendar / Cozi

A shared calendar handles the "when," but none of the "how." It doesn't know that this Thursday appointment needs a Grab booked at 2:30pm, that the driver needs a dementia briefing, that the part-time helper needs to be downstairs by 2:45, that HuiN needs to leave at 2:20 to make the rendezvous.

The gap: calendars are passive. Caregivers don't need a reminder — they need an assistant that takes action when the reminder fires.

03

Why not retrain Grab drivers?

A driver-side fix

Training drivers to handle elderly or dementia passengers would help — and we think Grab should do it (see For Grab). But driver training only kicks in once the passenger is already in the car. It doesn't fix the destination ambiguity, the scheduling burden, the multi-party coordination, or the fact that the caregiver is the one absorbing all of it before the ride even begins.

The gap: the pain is upstream of the ride. Better drivers don't reduce the dispatcher work; they just make the ride itself smoother.

04

Why not a voice assistant?

"Hey Siri, book a Grab for Mum"

Voice is great for fast individual actions — booking one ride, setting one reminder. It's terrible at the thing that actually breaks caregivers: orchestrating multiple parties across a thirty-minute window, monitoring exceptions, sequencing two arrivals at a clinic. That kind of work needs state, a UI, and an ambient eye on what's happening — none of which voice does well.

The gap: voice is a fast input. The caregiver problem is a slow choreography.

05

Why not just WhatsApp?

The status quo

Right now caregivers are the WhatsApp. They're the message bus between part-time helper, Grab driver, family member, receiving helper. They re-type the same dementia briefing each trip. They paste addresses they had to find themselves. They monitor the route and then forward the ETA manually.

The gap: WhatsApp is the problem in its purest form. The whole point of Carebear is to take the caregiver out of the message-bus role.

06

Trade-offs we're accepting

What we're knowingly leaving on the table — for now

Because Carebear lives outside Grab, the driver briefing has to be sent via Grab's in-app chat instead of appearing as a native briefing card in the driver's app. Live monitoring relies on Grab's shareable trip link rather than a real-time data feed. Booking takes one extra confirmation tap inside Grab.

These are all bridgeable the day Grab partners with us. We've designed Carebear so the upgrade path is "flip a switch," not "rebuild the product."
